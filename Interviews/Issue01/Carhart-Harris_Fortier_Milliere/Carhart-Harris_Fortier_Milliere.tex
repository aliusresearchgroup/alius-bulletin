% \section*{Abstract}
% This interview with Robin Carhart-Harris covers conceptual and methodological questions in neuroscientific research on psychedelics. Carhart-Harris discusses how dissatisfaction with the empirical limits of psychoanalysis led him to consider psychedelic drugs as a route to deep mental structures, and to pursue neuroimaging studies of psilocybin and LSD. The conversation turns to neuropsychoanalysis and his mapping of the default-mode network, particularly its subcortical and paralimbic components, onto the Freudian ego and unconscious. He addresses the state of consciousness research, the imprecision of its core definitions, and a shift of focus toward self-consciousness as the distinctive feature of the human mind. Further sections take up methodological issues in psychedelic neurophenomenology: the polysemy of terms such as supernaturalness in subjective questionnaires, the debate over whether drug-induced ego dissolution is best understood as a disruption of the narrative self or the minimal self, and the interpretation of default-mode network and task-positive network decoupling as a neural signature of the collapse of dualities. The interview examines entropy, criticality, and the relation between Karl Friston's free-energy principle and the reducing valve model for altered states of consciousness associated with Henri Bergson, Charlie Dunbar Broad, and Aldous Huxley.\par
